Student Off-campus Living Distribution


At the city scale, the objective is to better understand students off-campus living distribution and population aggregation, mapping the demographic density of students at the urban scale is a necessary method.

This interactive map not only provides the student demographic density in various areas of Boston, but also takes it with family and non-family household density in 2000 as the control group, so as to observe the trajectory of density distribution changes in the past ten years. Also, by superimposing the current student population density with university and college campus distribution, commuter rail network, and other environmental factors, students' preference in renting can be roughly inferred.

Firstly, based on the 2010 census data, we counted the total number of students living in each neighborhood and the total number of family household, respectively. The neighborhoods with the largest student population are Allston, Brighton, Dorchester; Jamaica Plain, Mission Hill and Fenway. The neighborhood with the most significant number of families are Dorchester, Roxbury, Jamaica Plain, Mission Hill. Nevertheless, the population number cannot directly demonstrate the specific demographic density and concentration of their living area.

To calculate the demographic density more accurately, we used the census block as the smallest counting unit, and the processed data were visualized to distinguish the differences of population density by gradient ramp. From the lightest yellow to the deepest blue represents the increase of student population density. If the blue area is larger, it also represents the larger number of students in this are.

Otherwise, if the color is lighter and smaller, it represents the lower student population density in the area. By mapping in the city scale, we found that the high-density student living areas are mostly found in Allston, Jamaica Plain, Fenway and South Boston, while Dorchester with a large number of students presents a low student living population density.

We also use flow diagram to integrate the data of neighborhood, density interval, and average rent price in the area for comparative analysis. Firstly, the result was consistent with mapping that the overall student density in Allston area was higher than 25%, while Jamaica Plain also had a 1/3 area with a student population density greater than 25%, but the other 2/3 areas had a lower demographic density. Whereas, the density of student in Dorchester is mainly concentrated at less than 25%, which implies that although the community has a relatively large number of students, its extensive land coverage, relatively scattered urban planning and lower living unit per house lead to a lower overall student population density than the other two areas with higher student population.

By comparing the student population density intervals with the overall average rent intervals, the average rent between 2901-3668 is mainly domain by the student demographic density between 25%-52%, which is much higher than that in the middle and low student density census blocks. As students can invite more roommates to share the rent, the average rent in some areas will rise relatively. Every unit rented to students shrinks the pool of housing available to Boston's workforce and - the pressure on the supply of unsubsidized affordable middle - income housing.
